> Part 3 of the [High Impact, Low Understanding](/blog) series.

## Three orientations

Every organization ships with some mix of priorities, whether they've said them out loud or not. In the AI era, I keep seeing the same three patterns:

*Solution Oriented.* Priority: make it work. Doesn't matter how. Prompt, generate, deploy. If the deliverable exists, the meeting is over.

*Velocity Oriented.* Priority: make it fast. Time to delivery. Number of projects launched. Output per quarter. The dashboard must go up and to the right.

*Security Oriented.* Priority: make it obscure. If nobody understands what was built, nobody can question it. We'll call this one "working as designed."

None of these are new. But AI poured gasoline on all three. And in traditional business environments where there's no technical review process and the person building is often the person approving, these orientations go unchallenged.

## Solution oriented: ship something, anything

I get it. Businesses need results. Leadership doesn't care about how something was built; they care about whether the deliverable exists. AI lets you produce that deliverable in a fraction of the time, and from a pure "does it work" standpoint, the output is usually fine.

But "fine" has a shelf life. A [December 2025 CodeRabbit analysis](https://www.coderabbit.ai/blog/2025-was-the-year-of-ai-speed-2026-will-be-the-year-of-ai-quality) of 470 open-source projects found that AI co-authored code contained 1.7x more major issues than human-written code. Logic errors were 75% more common. Security vulnerabilities were 2.74x higher. [45% of AI-generated code contains security flaws](https://www.darkreading.com/application-security/ai-generated-code-leading-expanded-technical-security-debt), according to Veracode's 2025 GenAI report.

That's the data from environments where trained engineers are at least looking at the output. In a traditional business setting, where the person who built the tool is the same person who approved it and nobody with a technical background ever saw it, those numbers are a floor, not a ceiling.

When "does it work" is the only question, the answer is always yes. Right up until it isn't.

## Velocity oriented: the dashboard must go up

In software teams with actual review processes, the data is already ugly. [Output volume increased 150%](https://www.coderabbit.ai/blog/2025-was-the-year-of-ai-speed-2026-will-be-the-year-of-ai-quality) over the past year. Bug counts went up 9% alongside it. [Code churn increased 39%](https://www.infoq.com/news/2025/11/ai-code-technical-debt/) in projects heavily using AI tools, meaning things are being built, shipped, and immediately rebuilt. [Maintenance and cleanup collapsed](https://thenewstack.io/vibe-coding-could-cause-catastrophic-explosions-in-2026/) by 60% since 2021. Nobody is going back to fix things because fixing things doesn't move the metrics.

Now think about that in a non-technical org where there are no metrics for quality at all. No code review. No QA process. No staging environment. Just a stakeholder who built something, showed it to their boss, and got a thumbs up because it looked like it worked. The velocity orientation in these environments isn't even measured. It's just cultural. Move fast. Show results. The quarterly review is coming.

The person who carefully evaluates the tool, stress-tests it with edge cases, and asks "what happens when this handles real customer data at scale" looks like they're stalling. The person who prompts their way to a demo in an afternoon looks like a star.

## The craftsmanship counter-narrative

There's a [good piece from Codurance](https://www.codurance.com/publications/software-craftsmanship-in-the-ai-era) that puts it plainly:

> "When speed is mistaken for progress, teams end up shipping more low-quality code, creating more rework, and compounding long-term risks."

Productivity has never been about volume of output. It's about whether you solved the right problem and got the solution right. That's always been the case. The AI era just made it easy to forget, especially in organizations that were never set up to evaluate technical work in the first place.

The tech industry seems to be catching on. CodeRabbit's framing, ["2025 was the year of AI speed; 2026 will be the year of AI quality,"](https://www.coderabbit.ai/blog/2025-was-the-year-of-ai-speed-2026-will-be-the-year-of-ai-quality) suggests the pendulum is swinging. But that's the tech industry talking to itself. Traditional businesses, the ones where AI adoption is happening fastest among non-technical staff, haven't even started having this conversation.

## The paradox

AI was supposed to free us to think harder about the interesting problems. Automate the tedious parts. Give people space to focus on strategy, design, and the stuff that actually requires human judgment.

Instead, for a lot of organizations, it incentivized thinking less about everything. The tedious work got automated, sure. But so did the judgment calls, the due diligence, the "should we even build this" conversations. Speed filled the vacuum that effort left behind.

I'm not anti-speed. Faster iteration is good. But speed is a means, not a mission. When velocity becomes the goal, you've already lost the thing that made the velocity worth having. And in orgs where nobody is asking "who reviewed this?", speed isn't just the goal. It's the only thing anyone can see.

Effort isn't overhead. It's the product.
