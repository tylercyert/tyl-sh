## The list that never ends

I keep a to-do list. Several, actually. At any given time I've got tasks scattered across work projects, personal projects, household stuff, creative ideas I swore I'd get to, and a growing backlog of "I should really learn that." Each list starts clean and motivated. Within a week it's thirty items long and I'm not looking at it anymore.

The problem was never a lack of things to do. It was the opposite. The sheer volume of tasks created this low-grade paralysis where I'd open the list, scan the wall of items, feel the weight of all of it, and close the app. Then I'd sit on my couch and do nothing. Not because I was lazy. Because every task felt equally urgent and equally impossible to start.

I've heard people call this "task paralysis." It's that state where having too much to do becomes functionally the same as having nothing to do, because the cognitive load of choosing where to start costs more energy than most of the tasks themselves.

## The backlog problem

I spent a long time thinking this was a discipline issue. Just pick something and do it. How hard is that? Turns out, pretty hard when you're staring at a flat list of forty items with no hierarchy, no priority, and no sense of what "done" looks like beyond clearing the whole thing.

The breakthrough came from work, not self-help. I'd been using agile workflows for years in engineering, sprints, backlogs, the idea that you pull a manageable number of tasks into a focused window and ignore the rest until the current batch is done. It's not revolutionary. But I'd never thought to apply it to my own life.

The agile backlog isn't a to-do list. It's a parking lot. Everything goes in, but you don't work from it directly. You pull a few things out, commit to those, and the rest stays out of sight until you're ready. The mental shift is subtle but real: you stop trying to hold the entire list in your head. You just work the current sprint.

## One task at a time

I started experimenting with this outside of work. Instead of maintaining a master list that I'd doom-scroll through, I'd pick one thing and commit to it. Not three things. One. The rule was simple: don't think about what's next until this one is done.

It worked better than I expected. The paralysis came from the weight of the whole list. Removing the list from view removed the weight. I wasn't ignoring my responsibilities. I was just looking at them one at a time instead of all at once.

But I didn't want another full-blown project management app for my personal life. Jira for groceries felt like overkill. I wanted something that matched the actual problem: I'm bored, I don't know what to do, and I need a single thing to focus on right now.

## Building the boredom coach

That's where [bored.tyl.sh](https://bored.tyl.sh) came from. The concept is simple. You load it up with tasks, organized into groups however you want. When you're sitting there wondering what to do with yourself, it asks you one question: are you bored?

Say yes, and it gives you a short countdown before revealing a task, a little buffer to mentally shift gears. Say no, and it skips the timer and hands you something immediately. Either way, you get one task. Not a list. Not a dashboard. One thing to go do.

You mark it done, and it celebrates that with you. Then it asks again. The whole interface is built around the idea that the decision of *what* to do is often harder than actually doing it. So it makes that decision for you.

There's a task manager behind it for when you want to add, reorder, or organize things. But the main experience is deliberately minimal. Open it, get a task, do the task. That's it.

## The point

Task paralysis isn't about being lazy or disorganized. It's about cognitive overload. The productivity industry loves to sell you better systems for managing more tasks, but sometimes the answer is simpler than that. Stop looking at the whole list. Pick one thing. Do it. Repeat.

Agile taught me that the backlog is supposed to be overwhelming. That's the whole point of having one, so the rest of your workflow doesn't have to be. The trick is building a wall between the backlog and the work, so you're never staring at both at the same time.

If any of this sounds familiar, [give it a try](https://bored.tyl.sh). It might help. At the very least, it'll give you something to do.
