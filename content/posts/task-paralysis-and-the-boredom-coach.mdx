## Nobody teaches you this

Task management has always been something of a passion for me because of the impact it's had on my life. But it wasn't something anyone sat me down and explained. Not in school, not in college, not at my first job. How to organize what you need to do, how to prioritize, how to avoid drowning in your own backlog — I had to figure all of that out on my own.

For a long time, I didn't have a system at all. I had lists. Lots of them. And every list started with good intentions and ended the same way: thirty items long, no hierarchy, no priority, and me closing the app because looking at it made me feel worse than not looking at it. That's task paralysis. When you have so much to do that the cognitive load of deciding where to start costs more energy than the tasks themselves. You sit on the couch and do nothing, not because you're lazy, but because everything feels equally urgent and equally impossible.

## Discovering agile

The turning point came from work. I started using agile workflows in engineering, sprints, backlogs, the idea that you pull a manageable number of tasks into a focused window and leave the rest alone until the current batch is done. Once I actually dove into frameworks like that, something clicked. I didn't just tolerate large projects anymore. I enjoyed tackling them.

The agile backlog isn't a to-do list. It's a parking lot. Everything goes in, but you don't work from it directly. You pull a few things out, commit to those, and the rest stays out of sight. The mental shift is subtle but real: you stop trying to hold the entire list in your head. You just work the current sprint. That separation between "everything I need to do" and "what I'm doing right now" was the whole game.

I loved these methodologies so much that I tried to bring them into my personal life. But it didn't translate. Agile tools are powerful, feature-rich, built for teams coordinating complex work. Running my household chores through Jira felt like driving a semi truck to the grocery store. I wanted something more lightweight. Something fun. Something potent enough to actually work but simple enough to not become its own chore.

## What actually works for me

Over the years I've figured out two techniques that consistently break me out of task paralysis at home.

The first is resetting. When I'm stuck, I do nothing on purpose. Stare at the wall. Sit on the ground. Meditate. Just exist for a few minutes with no input and no agenda. It sounds counterproductive, but every time I come out of it I have this huge sense of clarity. The fog lifts, priorities sort themselves, and I can actually move. It's like rebooting your brain.

The second is what I call kickstarter tasks. These are things I know are easy, things I can just do without any friction. The trick is that finishing one easy task creates enough momentum to roll into the next one, even the ones I've been avoiding. Cleaning the litter box might motivate me to finally clean the bathroom. Wiping down the kitchen counter leads to doing the dishes, leads to organizing the pantry. It starts a chain reaction.

This doesn't just apply to cleaning. It works for creative and development work too. Writing one small function, sketching one rough idea, fixing one minor bug. The momentum carries.

## Building the boredom coach

That's the whole idea behind [Are You Bored?](https://bored.tyl.sh). It's a fun, minimal way to manage your tasks without the overhead of a full project management tool. You open it and it asks you one question: are you bored?

Say yes, and it gives you a short countdown before revealing a task, a little buffer to reset and mentally shift gears. Say no, and it hands you something right away. Either way, you get one task. Not a list. Not a dashboard. One thing to go do.

The interface is intentionally minimal but visually engaging. I wanted it to give just enough stimulus to be fun without being distracting. It celebrates when you finish something, then asks again. The whole experience is built around the idea that deciding *what* to do is usually harder than doing it. So it makes that decision for you.

I've built in a preset list of common kickstarter tasks, the ones that have worked for me, so the app is useful right out of the box. But it also supports creating your own lists. Build a morning routine, a set of kickstarter tasks for when you're stuck, or even drop in your professional task list. Organize them into groups however you want.

This was a really fun project to build. Task management has genuinely changed my life, and I wanted to share something lightweight enough that anyone could pick it up without learning a methodology or reading a manual. Not everyone needs sprints and story points. Sometimes you just need someone to hand you a task and say "go do this."

If any of that sounds familiar, [give it a try](https://bored.tyl.sh). I hope it helps.
